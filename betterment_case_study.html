01
The 60-Day Plan
Assessment & Foundation

The first thing I do when I step into a new function is resist the urge to fix things before I understand them. Month 1 is about building the picture. Month 2 is about building the foundation.

Month 1
Listen & Assess
The team. 1:1s with all 5 in week one, not to evaluate but to understand what they're carrying, where they feel stuck, and what they already know that isn't documented anywhere. I also want to understand the current workload split and whether the team is set up for success before drawing any conclusions.
The business. Structured listening sessions starting with engineering leadership, the CFO, and the Head of People to understand what a great TA partnership looks like from their seat. From there, I expand to team leads and managers across functions, building 1:1 relationships as I learn the business and its structure.
Engineering data. Deep dive into the 85-day TTF before drawing any conclusions. Is it a process issue? Calibration? Comp misalignment? A small candidate pool? The data tells the story. I diagnose before I prescribe.
Infrastructure audit. Full Greenhouse review: pipeline stage consistency, scorecard completion, reporting gaps. Baseline metrics pulled: TTF by function and level, offer acceptance rate, pipeline conversion by stage, recruiter load per req.
Headcount model. Pull the full 2026-2027 plan across all functions. Build a capacity model: given hire volume, average TTF, and reasonable recruiter load ratios, what does the team actually need to look like to hit the targets? This is a business conversation, not just a TA planning exercise.
Month 2
Build the Foundation
Team structure and capacity. If the team is stretched thin, I'm not going to ask them to also build proactive pipelines on top of full req loads. I explore whether one of the tech recruiters can shift into a dedicated SWAT sourcing role for engineering — smart reallocation before a headcount ask. If the numbers don't work, I make the case to leadership with the model behind it.
Engineering: solve the actual problem. Once the data is clear, I work with engineering leadership to address it directly. If it's process, we redesign it. If it's calibration, we run a hiring bar session. If it's pipeline, we build one. If it's comp, I work with Total Rewards to understand where we stand.
Greenhouse cleanup. Standardize stages, build scorecards for top-volume roles, stand up dashboards in Looker Studio via Google Workspace. Leadership shouldn't need to ask for a recruiting update — the data should be visible at any time.
Sourcing playbook. Lean but usable: channel guidance by role type, NovaBridge-voice messaging, qualified-candidate profiles across all five functions. Stops the team from reinventing this on every req.
HM alignment. Role intake process that gets used because it saves hiring managers time. Biweekly pipeline updates to 80%+ of active hiring managers, brief, data-backed, and actionable. As relationships are built and cadence is formed, we shift those updates to Slack for faster, more natural communication.
60-day deliverable. 12-month TA strategy presented to the Head of People and relevant execs. Includes the capacity model, phased hiring plan by function and quarter, metrics framework, and a roadmap for what comes next.
02
Data-Driven Recruiting Strategy
Metrics & Operations

A data-driven recruiting function isn't one that tracks everything. It's one that tracks the right things, understands what the numbers are saying, and uses that to make better decisions.

Metrics That Drive Action
TTF by function and level. Company averages hide the real story. A 30-day fill for a G&A coordinator and a 30-day fill for a Staff Engineer are completely different situations. Segment by function and seniority to see where friction actually exists.
Pipeline conversion by stage. Where are candidates dropping? Each transition tells you something different: a drop at screen is a sourcing profile issue; a drop at panel is almost always calibration or process design. You can't fix what you can't locate.
Offer acceptance rate. Below 85% is a signal. Comp, candidate experience, or slow offers — each has a different fix. The metric tells you there's a problem; the diagnosis tells you what to do about it.
Recruiter capacity and load. Are recruiters set up for success? Understanding what the team can realistically carry helps ensure the right support, resources, and req distribution are in place before anything else is asked of them.
Time in stage. Where are candidates sitting? A req open for 60 days with candidates stuck in "interview scheduled" isn't a sourcing problem. It's a scheduling or decision-making problem. Makes the invisible visible.
Inclusive Hiring Strategy
Goals set by function and level. Set with the Head of People and business leaders, anchored in where actual gaps exist, not set in the abstract. The starting point is an honest look at where we are today and where the gaps are most pronounced.
Track at every stage. Representation at application, screen, panel, and offer. If underrepresented candidates are dropping at the phone screen, that's a process problem. If they're not applying at all, that's a sourcing and brand problem. Where it drops tells you what to fix.
Diverse slates as standard. Required before any role moves to panel. Not a checkbox — a signal that the search was done with a full picture of who's available in the market.
JD audits and channel expansion. Remove credential inflation and exclusionary language. Go beyond LinkedIn to fintech communities, HBCUs, and coding bootcamps. Work internally to formalize an EVP and build on the company's culture, so the story we tell externally is grounded and credible.
Structured interviews. Calibrated rubrics for every panel. Training is a requirement to participate, not a suggestion. This is both a fairness mechanism and a quality mechanism.
Accountability in the data. Diversity metrics live in the same quarterly business review as all other recruiting KPIs. Not a separate conversation — part of the standard operating cadence.
03
Building Trust with Senior Stakeholders
Influence & Autonomy

Trust with senior leadership isn't just built through relationship management. It's also built through relevance. Each stakeholder has a different definition of "TA is working" — and the fastest way to lose credibility is to show up with the wrong conversation. They need to trust that I'm driving toward the same goals.

Engineering Leadership
Start with a working session to understand their world before prescribing anything.
Deep dive into the 85-day TTF data. Diagnose before acting. Come with the right questions, not a solution.
Move fast on one visible fix. Early progress builds more trust than any relationship conversation.
Give them a live pipeline dashboard. They stop chasing TA when they can see what's happening without asking.
If a search runs long, explain why with market data, comp context, or interview bandwidth, and bring options, not just an update.
CFO
Speak the language of predictability and cost. That's how TA earns a seat in conversations most TA leaders never get invited to.
Build a workforce planning model that connects headcount to budget before spending happens, not retrospectively.
Make the cost of vacancy visible: delayed product work, overtime, contractor spend. An open Sr. Engineer role has a real dollar cost.
Frame TA as risk management, not a service function.
Never ask for budget without a business case. Never bring a problem without options.
CEO
One question they care about: can the company execute on the growth plan?
Keep communication strategic and tight. No TA process detail. No metrics deep dives.
At 60 days: here's the hiring plan for the next two quarters, the risks I'm managing, and what I need from the business.
Build trust as someone who doesn't create noise, surfaces the right issues at the right level, and runs the function without needing to be managed.
Head of People
Stretched thin — my job is to make their life easier, not add to their load.
They hear about issues from me before anyone else escalates. If engineering is frustrated, that conversation happens at my level first.
Standing biweekly check-in with a shared Google Doc agenda: async, focused, no surprises.
I bring judgment and decisions to check-ins, not status updates they could have pulled themselves from the dashboard.
On Autonomy
Communicate proactively, not reactively. Surface problems early with context and options. Make confident decisions within my lane and be clear about where my lane ends. The leaders who gave me the most room to operate over my career did so because I demonstrated early that I wouldn't let something blow up without warning. That's the trust I aim to build here.
Bonus
Beyond the 60 Days — Two areas I'd invest in once the foundation is stable. Not part of the core prompt, but part of how I think about building a TA function that compounds over time.
AI Stack
Month 6+

We don't touch AI tooling until the process works without it. AI on a broken process doesn't accelerate recruiting. It accelerates the wrong things faster.

01
Sourcing and pipeline intelligence. Signal-based matching to surface candidates the team wouldn't find through manual search. Multiplies sourcing capacity without adding headcount. Must integrate with Greenhouse and allow auditing of what it surfaces.
02
Workflow automation. Interview scheduling, candidate communications, status updates. Automates tasks that eat recruiter time without requiring recruiter judgment, freeing capacity for relationship and offer work.
03
Intelligence and decision support. Comp benchmarking against live market data, predictive TTF modeling, offer acceptance likelihood, diversity funnel analytics. Most sophisticated layer, introduced last, and only as good as the data behind it.
Change management: pilot with the willing, measure adoption not just output, build AI fluency as a team capability, not a personal initiative.
Employer Brand
Month 3+

The first step is building on the company's culture and formalizing an EVP internally, so whatever we tell candidates externally is grounded and credible.

Owned content. Employee spotlights, engineering "day in the life," leadership voices on LinkedIn. A careers page that converts and reflects the company's product sensibility.
Community presence. Women in Fintech, Fintech Diversity Radar, NYC fintech meetups, HBCU engineering programs. Show up consistently in 2-3 communities rather than sponsoring everything.
Candidate experience. Clear comms at every stage. Prepared interviewers. Human rejections. Fast offers. Doesn't need a budget; it needs a standard the team holds itself to.
Glassdoor engagement. Respond to reviews with a human voice, positive and critical. Companies that engage signal that leadership is paying attention.
Referral program. At companies with strong retention, the best source of hire is almost always employees who want to bring in people they trust.
Measure it. Careers page conversion, source-of-hire trends, offer acceptance rate, candidate experience scores. Brand compounds over 2-3 quarters of consistent effort.
